*Batchelor, P. (2019). ‘Grasping the intimate immensity: acousmatic compositional techniques in sound art as something to hold on to’, *Organised Sound*, 24(3). *
doi: [10.1017/S1355771819000372](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/1907DFDF68BF43506AD22562ACFECF7C/S1355771819000372a.pdf/grasping-the-intimate-immensity-acousmatic-compositional-techniques-in-sound-art-as-something-to-hold-on-to.pdf)  
[Download (author copy)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/12X5j_UrRqUxxfS8bAUsFdmZkoaG6ij5Y/view?usp=share_link)

Abstract: This article explores the accessibility of acousmatic compositional approaches within sound and installation art, focusing on the role of intimacy as a means of connecting with an audience. It proposes that installations can foster accessibility in two key ways: by cultivating relationships between installation and visitor, and by encouraging relationships between visitors themselves.

A range of acousmatic compositional techniques relating to intimacy are examined as strategies for drawing listeners into a work, particularly in relation to space and spatial relationships. These include recording approaches, the selection of sound materials, and the design of spatial environments and listening conditions.

Alongside a number of sound art examples, the author’s ongoing *GRIDs* series of sound sculptures is presented as a case study through which these concerns are articulated within an acousmatic aesthetic.

Batchelor, P. (2015). ‘Acousmatic approaches to the construction of image and space in sound art’, *Organised Sound*, 20(2), pp. 148–159.  DOI: [10.1017/S1355771815000035 ](https://doi.org/10.1017/S1355771815000035)  

[Download (author copy)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1K390GF9LnRt39xUZsaHuzCu2m7zWO81X/view?usp=share_link)

Abstract: This article examines concepts of image and space in both acousmatic music and sound art, identifying overlaps that are often overlooked in tendencies to treat these practices as incompatible. It focuses on two key areas: compositional concerns—particularly mimesis, image construction, and what is termed ‘ephemeral narrative’—and presentational strategies, especially multichannel speaker deployment.

Through discussion of relevant works, the article introduces the *GRIDs* project: a series of multichannel sound sculptures arranged in geometric arrays of numerous loudspeakers (in some cases hundreds). These configurations enable the creation of highly intricate spatial sound environments—fabricated landscapes—emerging directly from an acousmatic compositional approach.

Due to their modes of presentation and context, however, these works offer listening experiences distinct from those of acousmatic music in concert settings. The article therefore concludes by exploring how listeners engage with constructed image space in such installations, particularly in relation to their visual and environmental context.
